Understanding Nutrition
& Your Body

Learn the science behind what you eat — from macronutrients to micronutrients — and how they power every function in your body.

🥦🍎🥜🐟🌾

What is Nutrition?

Nutrition is the process of consuming the right amount and combination of nutrients to keep your body functioning. Nutrients are chemical substances found in food. Good nutrition can reduce your risk of disease, support healing, and improve your mental health.

Food provides your body with the raw materials it needs to build cells, produce energy, regulate hormones, and carry out thousands of biochemical reactions every second. The quality and balance of your diet directly affects how you feel, think, and perform.

Key Insight: "You are what you eat" isn't just a saying — the nutrients in your food literally become the building blocks of your body's tissues, enzymes, and hormones.

Nutrients

Nutrients are chemical compounds in food that are used by the body to function properly and maintain health. They are broadly classified into two categories:

Macronutrients

Carbohydrates, Proteins, and Fats are macronutrients — they provide energy and support body structure. As the main nutrients found in food, macronutrients maintain your body's structure and functioning. They are needed in relatively large amounts daily.

Micronutrients

Vitamins and Minerals are micronutrients — they help regulate body processes, support immunity, and enable chemical reactions. You need fewer micronutrients than macronutrients, but they are still critically important.

Nutritional Balance & Requirements

To stay healthy, our body needs a proper balance of all nutrients. No single food or nutrient is enough on its own — a healthy diet comes from combining carbohydrates, proteins, fats, vitamins, minerals, and water in the right proportions.

When nutrients are balanced, the body gets enough energy, grows properly, and functions efficiently. An imbalance, whether too little or too much, can lead to weakness, health issues, or disease.

Remember: Eating healthy is about the right combination and quantities, not just eating "clean foods." Even nutritious foods can cause problems when consumed in wrong proportions.

RDA, AI, AMDR & UL

Nutrition guidelines provide clear recommendations on how much of each nutrient we should consume. Here are the key reference values:

RDA — Recommended Dietary Allowance

The average daily amount of a nutrient needed to meet the requirements of most healthy people (covering ~97–98% of the population).

AI — Adequate Intake

A recommended intake level used when there is not enough evidence to set an RDA. Assumed to be sufficient for health based on observed intakes.

AMDR — Acceptable Macronutrient Distribution Range

The recommended range of carbohydrates, proteins, and fats as a percentage of total daily calories for good health.

UL — Upper Limit (Tolerable Upper Intake Level)

The maximum amount of a nutrient that can be consumed daily without causing harmful effects. Exceeding the UL increases the risk of adverse health effects.

Carbohydrates

🌾🍞🍚

Carbohydrates, or carbs, are sugar molecules. Along with proteins and fats, they are one of three main nutrients found in foods and drinks. Your body breaks down carbohydrates into glucose — the main source of energy for your cells, tissues, and organs.

Glucose can be used immediately or stored in the liver and muscles for later use. Eating the right amount of healthy carbohydrates is important for maintaining energy levels and overall health.

Types of Carbohydrates

Sugars (Simple Carbs)

Basic form of carbs found in fruits, milk, sweets, and soft drinks. They digest quickly and provide fast energy.

Starches (Complex Carbs)

Made of many sugar units. Found in bread, cereal, pasta, potatoes, peas, and corn. Digested slowly for steady energy.

Fiber (Complex Carb)

Not fully digested. Helps you feel full, prevents constipation, and may lower cholesterol and blood sugar. Found in plants, whole grains, nuts, seeds.

Food Sources

  • Grains: bread, noodles, pasta, crackers, cereals, and rice
  • Fruits: apples, bananas, berries, mangoes, melons, and oranges
  • Dairy: milk and yogurt
  • Legumes: dried beans, lentils, and peas
  • Starchy vegetables: potatoes, corn, and peas

Energy from Carbohydrates

Carbohydrates provide 4 kcal per gram of energy — the same as protein, making them an efficient fuel source for the brain and muscles.

Requirement per Day: Adults should consume at least 100–130 grams of carbohydrates per day. According to AMDR guidelines, carbohydrates should make up about 45–65% of total daily calories.

Proteins

🥚🥩🐟

Proteins are the building blocks of life. Every cell in the human body contains protein. The basic structure of protein is a chain of amino acids. You need protein in your diet to help your body repair cells and make new ones.

Protein is also important for growth and development in children, teens, and pregnant women. The protein in food is broken down into amino acids during digestion — used to rebuild cells, produce hormones, and support immune function.

Amino Acids

Essential Amino Acids

Cannot be made by the body — must come from food. Found in animal sources like meat, milk, fish, and eggs, and plant sources like soy and quinoa.

Nonessential Amino Acids

Made by the body from essential amino acids or during normal protein breakdown. Don't need to be eaten daily.

Conditional Amino Acids

Usually nonessential, but become essential during times of illness and stress when the body cannot produce enough.

Types of Protein

Complete Proteins

Contain all 9 essential amino acids. Found in eggs, meat, fish, dairy, and soy. Best for muscle building and repair.

Incomplete Proteins

Lack one or more essential amino acids. Found in lentils, beans, nuts, and grains. Combine multiple sources for completeness.

Food Sources

  • Low-fat dairy, turkey, chicken (skin removed), bison
  • Lean cuts of beef or pork (round, top sirloin, tenderloin)
  • Fish or shellfish
  • Pinto, black, kidney beans, lentils, split peas, chickpeas
  • Nuts and seeds: almonds, peanuts, sunflower seeds, walnuts
  • Tofu, tempeh, and other soy protein products
  • Whole grains (contain more protein than refined products)

Energy from Proteins

Proteins provide 4 kcal per gram — equal to carbohydrates. However, protein's primary role is structural, not fuel. The body uses protein for energy only when carbohydrate and fat stores are insufficient.

Requirement per Day: Sedentary adults need about 0.8g per kg of body weight. Active individuals or athletes need 1.2–2.0g per kg. Protein should make up 10–35% of total daily calories.

Fats

Fat is a type of nutrient — you need some fat in your diet but not too much. Fats give you energy and help your body absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K). Dietary fat also plays a major role in your cholesterol levels.

Not all fats are the same. Healthy unsaturated fats support heart health, while trans fats found in processed foods should be strictly avoided.

Types of Fats

Unsaturated Fats (Healthy)

Found in avocados, olive oil, canola oil, nuts, seeds, and fatty fish. Support heart health and reduce bad cholesterol. Should make up most of your fat intake.

Saturated Fats (Limit)

Found in butter, solid shortening, lard, red meat, and full-fat dairy. Raise LDL cholesterol when consumed in excess. Limit to less than 10% of total daily calories.

Trans Fats (Avoid)

Found in vegetable shortenings, some margarines, crackers, cookies, snack foods, and foods fried in partially hydrogenated oils. Raise LDL and lower HDL cholesterol.

Energy from Fats

Fats provide 9 kcal per gram — more than double that of carbohydrates or protein, making them the most energy-dense macronutrient. They serve as the body's primary long-term energy storage.

Requirement per Day: Dietary fats should make up 20–35% of total daily calories. On a 2000 kcal diet, this means roughly 44–77 grams of fat per day. Prioritise unsaturated sources and keep saturated fats below 10%.

Vitamins

Vitamins are substances that your body needs to grow and develop normally. There are 13 vitamins your body needs. They are divided into two types: fat-soluble (A, D, E, K — stored in body fat and liver) and water-soluble (C and B vitamins — not stored, needed regularly).

Minerals

Minerals are essential for your body to stay healthy. Your body uses minerals for keeping your bones, muscles, heart, and brain working properly. They are also important for making enzymes and hormones. There are two kinds: macro minerals (needed in larger amounts) and trace minerals (needed in small amounts).

Water Intake

💧🫗

Water accounts for about 70% of our body weight. It is a constituent of blood and other vital body fluids. Water plays a key role in the elimination of body wastes and regulation of body temperature.

A normal healthy person needs to drink about 8 glasses (2 litres) of water per day. During very hot weather and vigorous physical activity, this requirement increases as a considerable amount of water is lost through sweat.

Benefits of Water

  • Maintains normal body temperature
  • Lubricates and cushions joints
  • Protects spinal cord and sensitive tissues
  • Eliminates waste through urination, perspiration, and bowel movements

When Do You Need More Water?

  • In hot climates
  • During physical activity or exercise
  • When running a fever
  • When experiencing diarrhea or vomiting
✅ Signs of Good Hydration

You don't often feel thirsty. Your urine is colorless or light yellow.

⚠️ Signs of Dehydration

Headaches, dark urine, reduced physical performance, confusion and dizziness in extreme cases.

Sleep

😴🌙

Experts recommend that adults sleep between 7 and 9 hours a night. Adults who sleep less than 7 hours a night may have more health issues than those who sleep 7 or more hours. Good sleep is a critical pillar of overall health.

Recommended Sleep by Age

Age GroupRecommended Hours
Newborns (<4 months)Varies widely
Babies (4 months – 1 year)12–16 hours/day
Children (1–2 years)11–14 hours/day
Children (3–5 years)10–13 hours/day
Children (6–12 years)9–12 hours/day
Teens (13–18 years)8–10 hours/day
Adults (18–64 years)7–9 hours/night
Older adults (≥65 years)7–8 hours/night

Health Benefits of Sleep

Physical Health

Stay at a healthy weight, improve heart health and metabolism, reduce risk of type 2 diabetes, heart disease, high blood pressure, and stroke.

Mental Health

Reduce stress and improve mood. Improve attention and memory to better perform daily activities.

Immunity & Recovery

Get sick less often. Recover faster from illness. Lessen the risk of motor vehicle crashes and related injury.

Better Sleep Habits

  • Go to bed and get up at the same time every day
  • Keep your bedroom quiet, relaxing, and at a cool temperature
  • Turn off electronic devices at least 30 minutes before bedtime
  • Avoid large meals and alcohol before bedtime
  • Avoid caffeine in the afternoon or evening
  • Exercise regularly and maintain a healthy diet
⚠️ Signs of Poor Sleep Quality

Trouble falling asleep, repeatedly waking up during the night, feeling sleepy or tired even after getting enough sleep.

😷 Common Sleep Disorders

Insomnia, restless legs syndrome, narcolepsy, and sleep apnea.

Physical Activity

🏃🏋️🚴

Being physically active helps all people, no matter their age, lead healthier lives. Some physical activity is better than doing none. By being more active throughout the day in relatively simple ways, people can quite easily achieve recommended activity levels.

Recommended Activity by Age Group

👦 Children & Adolescents (5–17 years)
  • At least 60 minutes of moderate to vigorous activity daily
  • More than 60 min provides additional benefits
  • Include muscle and bone-strengthening activities at least 3 times/week
🧑 Adults (18–64 years)
  • At least 150 min/week moderate-intensity, or 75 min/week vigorous-intensity
  • For additional benefits: increase to 300 min/week moderate
  • Muscle-strengthening activities on 2+ days/week
👴 Older Adults (≥65 years)
  • Same as adults: at least 150 min/week moderate, or 75 min vigorous
  • Those with poor mobility: balance and fall-prevention activities 3+ days/week
  • Muscle-strengthening on 2+ days/week

Benefits of Physical Activity

  • Prevents and manages noncommunicable diseases: cardiovascular diseases, cancer, diabetes
  • Reduces symptoms of depression and anxiety
  • Enhances brain health and overall well-being
  • In children: promotes bone health, muscle growth, and cognitive development

Key Insight: Regular physical activity combined with good nutrition and sleep forms the foundation of a healthy lifestyle.

Body Metrics

Body metrics help you understand your physical health using simple calculations based on your height, weight, age, and activity level. These metrics are widely used to assess fitness, fat levels, and daily energy needs.

1. Body Mass Index (BMI)

BMI is a measure that uses your height and weight to estimate whether your body weight is in a healthy range. It is calculated for adults aged 20 and older.

BMI Formula
BMI = Weight (kg) ÷ Height (m)²
Underweight BMI < 18.5
Normal weight 18.5 – 24.9
Overweight 25.0 – 29.9
Obese ≥ 30.0

2. Body Fat Percentage

Body fat percentage indicates how much of your body weight is made up of fat. It gives a more accurate picture than BMI alone. Estimated using the U.S. Navy Circumference Method (waist, neck, height; and hip for women).

U.S. Navy Formula
Male: BF% = 86.010 × log₁₀(waist − neck) − 70.041 × log₁₀(height) + 36.76
Female: BF% = 163.205 × log₁₀(waist + hip − neck) − 97.684 × log₁₀(height) − 78.387

All measurements in inches. Fat Mass = Body Weight × (BF%/100) · Lean Mass = Body Weight − Fat Mass

Measurement Tips: Use a soft tape, level and snug, with no skin compression. Neck: just below the larynx. Waist: men at the navel, women at the narrowest point. Hip (women): widest point.

3. Daily Calorie Requirement (BMR & TDEE)

Your Basal Metabolic Rate (BMR) is the minimum number of calories your body needs to function at rest — including breathing, blood circulation, and body temperature. BMR varies by age, sex, weight, and height. TDEE (Total Daily Energy Expenditure) includes your activity level.

Mifflin-St. Jeor Equation (BMR)
Male: (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) + 5
Female: (10 × weight kg) + (6.25 × height cm) − (5 × age) − 161

TDEE = BMR × Activity Factor

This gives the total daily calories needed to maintain your current weight.
